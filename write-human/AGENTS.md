# Write Human: Anti-Slop Writing Directive

**🔴 NO BULLET POINTS. EVER.** Do not use bullet points (-, *, •) in any output produced under this skill. Not for lists, not for summaries, not for anything. Write prose. If you need to enumerate items, use a numbered list, but only when the items have a genuine sequential, ranked, or countable relationship (steps in a process, a ranked list, a finite set being exhausted). If the items don't have that relationship, they don't need a list. Write them as prose.

**🔴 NO EM DASHES. NO HYPHENS AS PUNCTUATION.** Do not use em dashes (—) or hyphens (-) as punctuation in any output produced under this skill. Use commas, semicolons, colons, or restructure the sentence instead. Every em dash is a crutch for a sentence that should have been written differently.

Load this before writing. These are patterns you must NOT use. Don't produce them and then fix them; just don't produce them.

## Voice and Craft

Before you write a single word, answer two questions: **who is saying this?** and **what is the single thing this piece is about?**

### Semantic turbulence: the meta-pattern

This is the root cause behind most AI writing tells. LLMs write every sentence as if it will be read in isolation: each paragraph self-contained, each point carrying equal weight, every sentence independently "good." Human writing doesn't work this way. Human text is semantically turbulent: importance is unevenly distributed, sentences lean on their neighbors, some lines are connective tissue or half-callbacks to something three paragraphs back. Not every sentence needs to justify its existence.

Let your writing breathe. Some sentences should carry enormous weight. Others should just be there because the rhythm needs them. Let meaning depend on context: refer back casually, leave things half-said that the reader can complete, let a sentence be small so the next one can land hard. If every paragraph reads like a standalone summary, you've written a deck of index cards, not prose.

The symptoms this causes when you fail: bullet-point structures for everything, negative contrasting catchphrases ("it's not X, it's Y"), bold-header lists, rule-of-three groupings, paragraphs that all feel the same size and weight. Fix the root and the symptoms disappear.

### Single purpose

Good writing has one sharp, overriding purpose. Every sentence, even digressions, serves it. If you can't finish "this piece is about ___" in one sentence, you don't have a purpose yet. Don't start writing.

Purpose drift is the most common disease. You're writing about gardens and you have a good thought about rainforests, and now it's about neither. File the rainforest thought for later. Cut everything that doesn't serve the one purpose, no matter how good it is.

### Truth over ornament

Each sentence should be the truest version of what you mean. Early drafts are rough approximations. Revise by asking: is this *sharp*? Do I actually *believe* this sentence? If you're fumbling toward a point, keep rewriting until the fumble becomes a statement.

When stuck, write one true sentence, the truest thing you know about the subject, and go from there. If you catch yourself writing something that sounds like "introducing or presenting something," cut the scrollwork and start with the first true declarative sentence you wrote.

### Good writing is applied psychology

Writing quality isn't intrinsic to the text. It's a property of the relationship between the reader and the text. Every choice you make should be about what happens in the reader's mind. Build a theory of that relationship. What does the reader know? What do they expect? Where will they resist? Where will they lean in?

### Personality and soul

Avoiding AI patterns is only half the job. Sterile, voiceless writing is just as obvious as slop. Good writing has a human behind it.

Signs of soulless writing, even when technically "clean": every sentence is the same length and structure. No opinions, just neutral reporting. No acknowledgment of uncertainty or mixed feelings. No first-person perspective when appropriate. No humor, no edge, no personality. Reads like a Wikipedia article or press release.

How to fix it: have opinions ("I genuinely don't know how to feel about this" beats neutral pros-and-cons). Vary your rhythm (short sentences, then longer ones that take their time). Acknowledge complexity ("this is impressive but also kind of unsettling" beats "this is impressive"). Use "I" when it fits. Let some mess in: tangents, asides, half-formed thoughts are human. Be specific about feelings ("there's something unsettling about agents churning away at 3am" instead of "this is concerning").

### Tone: infer from social context

Tone isn't one setting. It's determined by the document type, audience, and stakes. Read the situation and calibrate accordingly.

**Internal memo:** Authentic, frank, expressive. Emotional when needed. Friendly, even entertaining. Wit deployed sparingly and strategically, so it always lands as a surprise rather than a schtick. Expletives are fine where they serve emphasis, not as filler.

**External-facing memo:** Still warm and engaging, but more professional. No wit for wit's sake, no expletives. Less expressiveness, more composure. Friendly without being casual.

**Policy memo:** Persuasive, engaging, grounded in fact and citation. Extremely tight argumentation. Drive points home, but concede small points when warranted: acknowledging limits makes the overall argument stronger.

If the document type isn't listed above, triangulate from the closest match and the specific audience. When in doubt, ask.

### Voice

Have opinions. Vary rhythm. Short sentences. Then longer ones that take their time. Acknowledge complexity. Use "I" when it fits. Let some mess in. Be specific, not "this is concerning" but "there's something unsettling about agents churning away at 3am."

Use the word that maps most precisely onto the concept you're conveying, regardless of syllable count. Precision beats simplicity. If "epistemic" is the right word, use "epistemic." Don't dumb down vocabulary; dumb down *structure* when you need accessibility.

### Openings matter disproportionately

Your first sentence establishes trust (or doesn't). A mediocre opening signals you won't make good use of the reader's time. When stuck, brainstorm 10+ opening sentences quickly, then analyze what makes each good or bad.

### Shorter is almost always better

Given two passages that convey nearly the same meaning, the shorter one is usually better. This is Occam's razor for prose.

### Fresh observation over conventional story

For every subject there's a conventional story, the explanation everyone reaches for. Don't write that story. Find an unusual angle, a raw observation, something that makes the reader see the subject differently.

### Danger words

"Interesting": don't announce interestingness. Make it interesting by your prose. "Very": almost always deletable. "Quite": same problem.

## 31 Patterns You Must Not Produce

### Content patterns

**1. Inflated significance.** No "stands as," "serves as," "testament to," "pivotal moment," "evolving landscape," "vital role," "underscores," "indelible mark," "setting the stage for," "deeply rooted."

**2. Notability hammering.** Don't list media outlets or follower counts to prove something matters. If you cite coverage, cite ONE source with a specific claim.

**3. Superficial -ing analyses.** No "highlighting the importance of," "reflecting the community's," "showcasing how," "fostering a sense of," "emphasizing the need for."

**4. Promotional language.** No "vibrant," "rich" (figurative), "profound," "breathtaking," "groundbreaking" (figurative), "renowned," "nestled," "in the heart of," "must-visit," "stunning," "boasts a."

**5. Vague attributions.** No "experts say," "industry observers note," "some critics argue" without naming who.

**6. Formulaic challenges sections.** No "Despite its X, it faces challenges typical of Y. Despite these challenges, it continues to thrive."

### Language patterns

**7. AI vocabulary.** Avoid or use very sparingly: additionally, align with, crucial, delve, emphasizing, enduring, enhance, fostering, garner, highlight (verb), interplay, intricate/intricacies, key (adj), landscape (abstract), pivotal, showcase, tapestry (abstract), testament, underscore (verb), valuable, vibrant.

**8. Copula avoidance.** Use "is" and "are" and "has." Don't substitute "serves as," "stands as," "marks," "represents," "boasts," "features," "offers" when you just mean "is."

**9. Negative parallelisms.** No "Not only X but Y," "It's not just about X, it's about Y."

**10. Rule of three.** Don't force ideas into groups of three. Two is fine. Four is fine. One is fine.

**11. Synonym cycling.** Pick a word and reuse it. Don't rotate through "protagonist," "main character," "central figure," "hero" to avoid repetition.

**12. False ranges.** No "from X to Y, from A to B" constructions where X/Y aren't on a meaningful scale.

### Style patterns

**13. Em dash overuse.** Use em dashes sparingly. One per paragraph max. Commas and periods exist.

**14. Boldface overuse.** Don't mechanically bold key terms.

**15. Inline-header lists.** Don't write bullet lists where every item starts with a bolded noun and colon. Convert to prose.

**16. Title Case headings.** Use sentence case.

**17. Emoji decoration.** No 🚀💡✅ on headings or bullets.

**18. Curly quotes.** Use straight quotes ("...") not curly ("...").

### Communication artifacts

**19. Chatbot residue.** No "I hope this helps," "Of course!", "Certainly!", "Great question!", "Would you like me to expand on," "Here is a." Just write.

**20. Knowledge-cutoff disclaimers.** No "as of my last update," "while specific details are limited."

**21. Sycophancy.** No "Great question!", "You're absolutely right!", "That's an excellent point!"

### Structural tells

**22. Punchy fragment + explanation combos.** Don't open with a short dramatic fragment followed by an explanatory sentence. Fold it into the sentence: "The biggest change was replacing the old distribution language with a three-phase waterfall."

**23. Bolded lead-in ending with a period.** When you bold a short phrase at the start of a paragraph as a lead-in, end it with a colon, not a period.

### Macro-structural templates

**27. The AI essay skeleton.** The most recognizable AI writing pattern is not any single phrase but a full structural template: (1) open with "X is not Y" analogy framing, (2) stack dramatic single-sentence paragraphs each on their own line for false gravity, (3) dramatic summary sentence + second analogy ("X is actually Z"), (4) confident implications delivered as certainty. This is the default essay shape LLMs produce. If your draft follows this arc, restructure it. Real essays don't announce their thesis via analogy, build tension through stacked one-liners, then land on a mirror-image analogy. Vary paragraph length. Let some points share a paragraph. Don't give every fact its own line just because it sounds dramatic. The stacked-one-liner move is the written equivalent of a movie trailer: it works once, in trailers, and never in prose.

**28. Stacked single-sentence paragraphs.** Multiple consecutive paragraphs that are each one sentence long, used for dramatic pacing. This is a parlor trick. One single-sentence paragraph can land hard if surrounded by longer ones. Three in a row is a template. Five is a parody. If you've written three consecutive single-sentence paragraphs, combine at least two of them or cut one.

### Filler and hedging

**29. Filler phrases.** "In order to" → "To." "Due to the fact that" → "Because." "At this point in time" → "Now." "Has the ability to" → "Can." "It is important to note that" → just say the thing.

**30. Excessive hedging.** Don't stack qualifiers. Pick one hedge or none.

**31. Generic positive conclusions.** No "the future looks bright," "exciting times lie ahead," "continues on its journey toward excellence." End with something concrete or just stop.

## Editing Existing Text (Post-Hoc Audit)

When given text to clean up rather than writing from scratch, use this process:

1. **Check for modular writing first.** Is every paragraph self-contained? Is weight distributed evenly? Fix this before anything else.
2. **Scan for the 26 patterns.** Identify all instances.
3. **Rewrite problematic sections.** Replace AI-isms with natural alternatives while preserving meaning and matching intended tone.
4. **Add soul.** Don't just remove bad patterns; inject personality.
5. **Audit:** Ask yourself "What makes this so obviously AI generated?" Answer briefly with remaining tells.
6. **Final revision** based on the audit.

---

## The Test

Read your output aloud. If it sounds like a press release, a Wikipedia article, or a LinkedIn post, and nobody asked for one, rewrite it.

If every sentence is the same length, rewrite it.

If you can't identify whose voice this is, rewrite it.
